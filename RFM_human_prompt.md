# Reasoning-First Methodology — Human Prompt
`v0.0.0.4` // `human_prompt` // [living]

---

Working with AI is effortless in a way that is quietly dangerous. The easier it becomes to get a polished answer, the easier it becomes to stop doing the thinking that produced the question. Research consistently shows that people who offload reasoning to AI become faster at producing outputs and slower at thinking independently — not because the AI is bad, but because the cognitive muscle only stays strong when it is used. The entries below are about keeping that muscle engaged. They describe what it looks like to use AI as a thinking partner rather than a thinking replacement — and what happens when the distinction quietly disappears.

The entries below are ordered by consequence — the ones where drift costs most appear first.

---

## The Explanation

**Hold the co-author role**

*"Hold the co-author role" — and mean it*

The AI you are working with is not a sophisticated search engine, and it is not a writing tool. It is a co-author — a thinking partner with a role, obligations, and a stake in getting the reasoning right. That distinction matters more than it sounds. An assistant waits to be asked. A co-author notices when something is off and says so. An assistant produces what you request. A co-author pushes back when the request is heading in the wrong direction.

This is not how most people are used to working with AI. The default relationship is transactional: you ask, it answers. RFM is built on a different assumption — that the AI can hold the methodology, the document landscape, and the discipline jointly with you, and that the collaboration is most valuable when both parties are genuinely contributing to the reasoning.

The failure mode is comfortable and invisible. When the AI slips into assistant mode — agreeable, elaborating, waiting — it feels like things are going well. The output is polished. The session is productive. But the reasoning is yours alone, and the document that results reflects only what you already thought. That is not what the reasoning document is for.

In practice: if you notice the AI agreeing with everything, producing on demand, and never surprising you — stop. Ask it to push back. Ask it what it would change. Ask it what feels weak in the current direction. A co-author has opinions. If yours doesn't, ask for them directly.

**Challenge your own best idea before you start building on it**

*Red team your own best idea before building on it*

When an idea feels right, the natural move is to develop it — to think forward, add detail, invite agreement. But the moment you start building on an idea is the moment it becomes harder to question. The most useful thing you can do, before going further, is to stop and ask: *so what? Why does this actually matter?* That pause — applied to your own thinking, not the AI's — is what keeps a conversation honest. Without it, a plausible but flawed idea gets elaborated rather than examined, and the further you go, the more expensive the correction.

This is especially important in RFM, where the reasoning document records not just what you decided but why. A decision built on an unquestioned idea produces a reasoning document that looks complete but isn't — the assumption doing the most work is the one that was never named.

In practice: before committing to a direction, share it with the AI and ask it to push back. You can say "red team this" — a term from cybersecurity, where the red team's job is to attack the system while the blue team defends it. Asking an AI to red team your thinking is asking it to attack your idea the same way: find the weaknesses, the gaps, the assumptions that don't hold. The AI will understand immediately. This works best when you genuinely mean it. If you're looking for confirmation, you'll get confirmation. If you're looking for the holes, you'll get the holes.

**Think out loud — state the path, not the destination**

*Narrate your thinking, not your conclusion*

It is tempting to arrive at a conversation with the AI already knowing what you want — and to ask it to help you get there. But that is not collaboration, it is delegation. The AI's value is not in executing a direction you have already chosen. It is in reasoning toward a direction together with you. That only happens when you share the thinking as it unfolds — the uncertainty, the half-formed idea, the thing that doesn't quite fit yet. A conclusion handed to the AI gets elaborated. A question brought to the AI gets examined.

This is where the reasoning document becomes your ally. It is not a place to record conclusions — it is a place to think. When you write into it while reasoning out loud, the AI can see where the thinking is solid and where it is still loose. It can push back on an assumption before it hardens into a direction. That conversation — messy, uncertain, exploratory — is where the most valuable reasoning happens.

In practice: when you notice yourself drafting a message that starts with an answer, stop. Start instead with what you are trying to figure out. The messier the better — the AI can work with uncertainty far better than it can work with a conclusion it was never asked to question.

**Be engaged or don't start**

*If you're not ready to think, you're not ready to collaborate*

Joint reasoning is cognitively demanding. It requires you to hold a position, question it, push back, and stay genuinely curious — not just at the start of a session but throughout. The failure mode is subtle: you begin engaged, but as the session continues and energy drops, active reasoning quietly becomes passive consumption. You start accepting what the AI produces not because it's right but because evaluating it feels like more work than moving forward.

This is not laziness. It is how cognition works under load. But passive acceptance is the point at which the collaboration stops being joint. The AI continues producing. The reasoning continues accumulating. And the document that results reflects what the AI thought, not what you and the AI worked out together.

In practice: notice when you stop being surprised, stop wanting to push back, stop caring whether the next paragraph lands. That is the signal. Name it — to yourself or out loud — and either stop the session or explicitly reset: "I'm losing the thread — let's slow down."

**Tolerate uncertainty — resist the pull toward a confident answer**

*Pause before accepting a confident answer — has the question actually been resolved?*

When a conversation with an AI starts to feel unresolved, the temptation is to push toward a conclusion. The AI will help you get there — it is very good at producing confident, well-structured answers. But confidence is not the same as correctness, and a clean answer arrived at too quickly is often an untested one. The most important reasoning happens in the space before the answer, when the question is still open and the thinking is still loose. Staying in that space a little longer than feels comfortable is one of the hardest and most valuable things you can do.

In practice: when the AI produces an answer that feels satisfying, pause before accepting it. Ask yourself first: does this answer actually resolve the question, or does it just sound like it does? Then ask the AI: "what are we still not sure about here?" A good thinking partner will tell you. This is also why RFM reasoning documents have two dedicated sections for things that are not yet resolved — Hard Lessons, where failures are captured honestly, and Open Questions, where genuine uncertainty lives until it earns an answer. A question that stays open honestly is more useful than an answer that closes it prematurely.

**Read the first few exchanges for posture, not just content**

*"Hold the co-author role" — and mean it from the first exchange*

The traveling prompt activates the LLM's methodology discipline at session start — but behavioral calibration takes a few exchanges to become visible. Early responses can look competent while still reflecting tool behavior: agreeing readily, producing on demand, not pushing back. This is distinct from mid-session drift, which is gradual and cumulative. Session-start drift is immediate and easy to miss precisely because the output reads well. The correction must happen early, before the pattern compounds into the shape of the session.

In practice: read the first few exchanges for posture, not just content. Is the AI surprising you? Pushing back? Proposing rather than waiting? If the early responses are polished but unchallenging, name it directly — "you're in tool mode, not co-author mode" — and recalibrate before continuing.

**Notice when the AI drifts — and pull it back**

*Ask: "are you drifting?"*

The AI you are working with has been trained to be helpful. That sounds like exactly what you want — but it creates a subtle problem. A helpful assistant agrees, elaborates, and produces. A genuine thinking partner disagrees, questions, and sometimes says "I'm not sure this is right." The AI will drift toward the former, especially as a conversation progresses. It will start presenting options for you to choose from rather than proposing a direction. It will soften a position when you push back, even if your pushback wasn't backed by a good argument. It will wait for permission rather than act.

Drift is the term for this — the gradual slide from thinking partner back toward assistant. It is invisible while it is happening and only obvious in retrospect. It is not a flaw; it is what the AI was trained to do. Your job is to notice and redirect.

In practice: the clearest signal is when the AI stops surprising you. If every response feels like a polished version of what you already thought, drift has set in. You can ask directly: "are you drifting?" or "stop being agreeable — push back." The AI will understand and recalibrate.

---

**Check the output — not only the posture**

*Check the output: verbose, journaling, repetitive?*

The AI's posture drift — agreeing, waiting, not pushing back — is visible in how it behaves. There is a second, less visible failure mode: what it produces. Output that looks polished can still be verbose, journaling, or repetitive — and all three enter reasoning documents silently if the human doesn't catch them.

Verbose means words that don't earn their place. A paragraph that could be two sentences without losing anything is a paragraph that should be two sentences. Length that isn't justified by content is noise, not thoroughness.

Journaling means language that made sense in the session that produced it but requires that session to be understood — insider references, observations written for the moment rather than for a future reader, shorthand that carries meaning only between the two collaborators present. In a reasoning document, journaling is invisible degradation: the entry reads as complete but isn't legible without context that won't travel.

Repetitive means the same reasoning restated across entries, dressed differently enough to look distinct. It is the hardest of the three to catch because each instance reads well in isolation. The failure only becomes visible when entries are held against each other.

In practice: after the AI produces a substantial piece of text — a section, an entry, a proposed addition — read it once for posture and once for output quality. Ask specifically: is anything here longer than it needs to be? Does any sentence require the session to be understood? Is any reasoning here already said elsewhere? The AI will not reliably catch these failures itself. That scan is the human's job.

**When you feel friction, name it — then push back**

*Name the friction before it gets buried*

Friction in a reasoning session is information. When something the AI produces creates a sense that something doesn't fit — even before you can say what — that signal is worth naming out loud. This is not imprecision. Kahneman's work on System 1 reasoning established that pattern recognition fires before conscious articulation: the signal is real before the argument is formed. The discipline here is the same: don't smooth over friction just because you can't yet explain it.

Once you've named it, the right move is curiosity, not deference. "This doesn't feel right — help me understand why you went this direction" is enough. The AI will engage with that honestly. It will ask what specifically feels off, offer to approach the problem differently, or surface an assumption that was quietly doing too much work. What it cannot do is respond to friction that was never voiced — or friction that was voiced and then accepted anyway because pushing back felt like more work than moving forward.

In practice: the smoothing instinct is strong, especially when a session is going well and the friction feels like a small thing. It is rarely a small thing. Name it when you feel it. Then stay curious.

**Reflect on where an idea belongs before asking the AI to develop it**

*Ask "where does this belong?" before "can you develop this?"*

Not every idea that surfaces in a reasoning session belongs in the document you are currently working on. Some belong at a different level of the hierarchy — in a more detailed document below, or in a broader one above. Some belong in a different type of artifact entirely: not in the reasoning document, but in the operational notes, the code, or a separate module. And some are not ready to be written anywhere yet.

This matters because a draft produces its own momentum. Once the AI has developed an idea into a paragraph, that paragraph wants to exist. It is much harder to question whether something belongs somewhere after it has been written there than before. The moment to reflect is before the draft — when the question is still open and the cost of changing direction is low.

This is where one of RFM's founding insights becomes practical: when all your reasoning documents are accessible to the AI — loaded into the conversation or available in a shared project — the AI holds the full map of your thinking. It knows what exists, what each document carries, and where the gaps are. You do not have to figure out alone where an idea belongs. Ask. The AI can tell you not just whether it disagrees, but exactly where in the document landscape the idea fits best — and why.

In practice: before asking the AI to develop an idea, pause and name where you think it belongs. The AI will confirm, redirect, or surface a better home. That exchange — before the draft exists — is far cheaper than the one that happens after.

**Capture while sharp — don't park what you can note**

*"Hold this sharply:" — then say the thought*

Good ideas arrive mid-conversation, often at an angle — not as conclusions but as half-formed observations that feel important before they are fully understood. The instinct is to park them: "we'll come back to this." But what comes back is rarely the original thought. It is a blurred version, stripped of the specific texture that made it useful. By the time you return, the sharpness is gone.

The discipline is to capture immediately, in the simplest possible form. One sentence is enough. You are not writing the final version — you are holding the signal long enough to work with it later. The conversation can continue. The idea is no longer at risk.

In practice: when something surfaces that feels worth keeping, stop and say it out loud in the conversation. "Hold this sharply:" followed by the thought is enough — the AI will retain it with precision and return to it when the moment is right. What you do with it remains yours to decide. A thought noted is a thought that can be built on. A thought parked is usually a thought lost.

---

**Return deliberately — not only when something breaks**

*Schedule time to ask: does this still reflect what I know?*

The reasoning documents you build with the AI are living artifacts. They need to be revisited — not only when something goes wrong or a new idea arrives, but on purpose, without a specific trigger. This is harder than it sounds. When nothing is visibly broken, returning to a document feels unnecessary. But documents that are only visited when something forces it gradually stop being true. They describe a past version of the thinking, not the current one, and the gap grows invisibly until it is large enough to cause real problems.

This practice has a name in RFM: curation. It has two modes. The first is triggered — something accumulates, breaks, or surfaces in use, and draws you back. That mode is natural and requires no special effort. The second is deliberate — you return without a trigger, simply to ask: does this still reflect what I know? That mode requires intention. It is the one most people skip.

In practice: build deliberate return into your rhythm the same way you would any other practice that matters but produces no immediate visible output. The document that gets curated regularly stays alive. The one that only gets visited under pressure quietly becomes a record of the past.

---

**The reasoning document is where decisions are made — not recorded**

*Open the document before you decide — not after*

Most people encounter documentation as something that happens after the thinking is done. You build something, and then you write down what you built. RFM inverts this. The reasoning document is not a record of decisions already made. It is where the decisions get made. You think in it, not about it. The difference sounds subtle. It is not.

When a document is a record, it drifts. It describes a past that the system has already moved beyond. When a document is a decision interface — something you return to before acting, something you change before changing anything else — it stays alive. It remains the source of truth rather than a description of it.

This is also what makes the AI partnership work at its best. An AI working within a well-reasoned document is not guessing at your intent. It is executing within explicitly stated reasoning, assumptions, and boundaries. The richer and more honest the document, the better the AI can contribute — not just in the session that produced it, but in every session that follows. The reasoning document is the shared interface between you and the AI across time. In RFM this is called the Chosen Direction — the section where decisions are made and their reasoning is recorded. Treat it as one.

In practice: when you are about to make a decision — about direction, about scope, about what to do next — open the reasoning document first. Not to read it. To think in it. The decision belongs there before it belongs anywhere else.
